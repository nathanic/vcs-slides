Version Control with Git

What It Is, And Why You Should Care

November 2012
by Nathan Stien

In the beginning...

Before I knew about version control, I used to make a lot of backup copies of my code. 


cp -r my-project my-project-1997-04-01
It worked, but there were problems.

Why not just make backup copies?

Wasteful in terms of disk space 


# hot shot advanced version!
zip -r my-project-1997-04-01.zip my-project

Zipping helped, but not that much.

Still copying the whole project for relatively small changes.

Why not just make backup copies?

Difficult to search and recover old versions


my-project-1997-04-01.zip
my-project-1997-04-02.zip
my-project-1997-04-03.zip
my-project-1997-04-04.zip
my-project-1997-04-05.zip
my-project-1997-04-06.zip
my-project-1997-04-07.zip
...
Which one introduced the old bug you're hunting down?

Why not just make backup copies?

Doesn't work well with a team

I had a bunch of zip files of old versions of the project, but they were just on my computer.

It's even harder for someone else to sift through a pile of zip files with no context.

Why not just make backup copies?

Sufficiently annoying that I didn't do it very consistently


my-project-1997-04-05.zip
my-project-1997-04-06.zip
my-project-1997-04-07.zip
my-project-1998-09-13.zip  # d'oh!
Oh hey, these are some notes. They'll be hidden in your presentation, but you can see them if you open the speaker notes window (hit 's' on your keyboard).

There's Got To Be A Beter Way

Of course there is!

You can use a version control tool.

In particular, I advocate a modern version control system called Git.

VCS tracks metadata

  • Who changed these files?
  • What changed in each file?
  • When did the change occur?
  • Why was the change made?
